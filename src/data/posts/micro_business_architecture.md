I've been kicking around an idea, so I'm going to try to put it into words even though it's not fully baked yet.

Here's the setup: AI has made it absurdly cheap to build things. Not cheap like "save a few hours" cheap — cheap like "one person can do what used to take a small team" cheap. Code, design, copy, data analysis, even basic ops stuff. The execution side of building a product is collapsing in cost so fast that it's hard to keep up with what's possible quarter to quarter.

And that's got me thinking about what happens next. Not to jobs — everyone's already having that conversation. I'm thinking about what happens to the idea of a _business_ itself.

## The thing I keep noticing

Most people I know who are building stuff with AI right now are stuck in one of two mental models. Either they're using it to be more productive at their day job, or they're trying to build the next big thing. Job or startup. Employee or founder. Those are the two options people seem to see.

But there's something in between that I think might be more interesting — and more realistic for most builders. Something like: a small, focused, self-sustaining business that one person can operate because AI handles the execution layer. Not a side hustle — those are unfocused by nature, just chasing whatever pays. And not a startup — I don't need venture money and I don't need to "scale" in the way VCs mean it. Something more deliberate than either of those.

I've been calling it Micro Business Architecture in my head, partly because I think the framing works and partly because the MBA acronym is too good to pass up.

## What I mean by "architecture"

The architecture part isn't just a fancy word. I think it's actually the key idea.

When I think about what makes this different from just "freelancing" or "building a side project," it comes down to design. You're not just making a thing and hoping someone pays for it. You're designing a small system — the product, the tooling around it, the workflows that keep it running, and maybe most importantly, the boundaries around what it _doesn't_ do.

That last part is the one I keep coming back to. If you're one person, scope is everything. The whole model falls apart if you let the thing grow past what one person plus AI can actually operate. So the architecture has to include hard edges. This is what the business does. This is what it doesn't. That discipline is what keeps it from turning into a second full-time job that doesn't pay enough.

## The MBA thing isn't just a joke

Okay, it's partly a joke. But there's a real point in there.

A traditional MBA teaches you how to operate inside large organizations. Resource allocation, team management, corporate strategy — it's a credential built for institutional life. And that made total sense when building anything required an institution. You needed people and capital and coordination just to get a product to market.

But if AI is genuinely collapsing that requirement — if one person really can build, ship, and operate a real product — then the skill set changes completely. You don't need to learn how to manage a team. You need to learn how to scope a problem, design something tight enough to run solo, figure out where AI actually helps versus where it just gives you confident-sounding garbage, and sustain the thing without burning out.

Nobody's teaching that. The people figuring it out are just... building things and learning as they go. Each one teaches you a little more about the pattern. That's the "curriculum," if you want to call it that.

## Where I think this could go

Here's the speculative part, and I want to be honest that I don't know if this plays out.

I think there might be a future where a certain kind of person — someone technical, someone with domain expertise in something specific, someone with the disposition to work without a lot of external structure — builds not one business like this, but two or three. Small ones, sharing some infrastructure, maybe overlapping in audience. Each one self-sustaining. Each one deliberately scoped. Together they look like a real business, but with way less fragility than a single venture because nothing depends on everything else.

Maybe that's overly optimistic. Maybe the execution layer doesn't collapse as far as I think it will, or maybe the operational overhead of even a "micro" business is higher than it looks from the outside. I genuinely don't know.

But the piece I feel pretty confident about is this: the gap between "I have an idea and I understand a problem" and "I'm running a business that solves that problem" is getting narrower fast. The execution part — the part that used to be the hard part — is becoming the easy part. What's left is the judgment. Knowing what to build, who it's for, and having the discipline to keep it small enough to actually work.

If that's true, then the most valuable skill isn't an MBA. It's the ability to architect something small, run it lean, and know when to stop adding to it.

I don't know if that's a movement or a moment. But I think it's a pattern worth paying attention to.
